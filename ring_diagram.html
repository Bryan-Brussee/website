    Asylum countries for Iran, Myanmar and Sudan
    (2013-2015)

    Built in collaboration with Roger Cohen of The New York Times, the above diagram shows where Iranian, stateless Rohingya and Sudanese refugees went after fleeing their home countries between 2013 and 2015. The visualization above is a repurposed version of code found here and uses data gathered from Australia's monthly OSB reports.

    When viewed this way, it becomes easy to see how the vast majority of asylum-seekers migrate based on geographic proximity. Chad, Myanmar and Bangladesh receive the most refugees, because they each neighbor one of the three countries in question. In contrast, the West receives relatively few of these people. Germany receives the most out of any country in the West yet still receive less than 2% of the total refugees from the three countries in question.

    Australia took in about 21,000 people between 2013 and 2015, less than half a percent of the world's total asylum seekers from Iran, Myanmar and Sudan. Some of these people ended up in Australia's island prisons.
